## The enforcement wave (this is the fear, with real numbers)

| Company | Date | Penalty | What they did wrong |
|---|---|---|---|
| **GoodRx** | Feb 1, 2023 | **$1.5M** | First-ever HBNR enforcement. Shared prescription/condition data with Facebook, Google, Criteo, Branch, Twilio for targeted ads; didn't report the disclosure. |
| **Premom** (Easy Healthcare) | May 17, 2023 | **$100K** + $100K to states | 2nd HBNR action. Shared health data with AppsFlyer and Google without consent. |
| **BetterHelp** | Order July 14, 2023 | **$7.8M** refunds | Shared email, IP, and **therapy-intake answers of ~1.5M users** with Facebook, Snapchat, Criteo, Pinterest. Order **bans sharing health data for ads** and bans retargeting. |
| **Cerebral** | Apr 15, 2024 | **$7M+** ($5.1M refunds + penalty) | Disclosed sensitive data of **~3.2M consumers** to LinkedIn, Snapchat, TikTok while promising "safe, secure, and discreet." |
| **Monument** (alcohol addiction) | Apr 2024 | $2.5M (suspended, inability to pay) | Sent Meta/Google **custom events like "Paid: Weekly Therapy"** + email/IP for ~84,000 users despite "100% confidential" promise. Banned from disclosing health data for ads. |

**The pattern in one sentence:** every one of these was a company sending health data to advertising
platforms through **tracking pixels and marketing APIs** — the exact marketing tech a normal practice
uses. Behavioral/mental-health and addiction firms dominate the list (BetterHelp, Cerebral, Monument),
which is why those verticals are the highest-risk.

## The FTC Health Breach Notification Rule (HBNR) & the 2024 amendments
- The HBNR requires certain organizations **NOT covered by HIPAA** — vendors of personal health records,
  PHR-related entities, and their service providers — to notify individuals, the FTC, and sometimes the
  media of a breach of unsecured identifiable health information.
- **Finalized April 26, 2024** (Federal Register May 30, 2024; effective July 29, 2024): expressly
  extends coverage to **non-HIPAA health apps and connected devices.**

## The "non-HIPAA trap" (the most important idea for the guide)
The amended HBNR **redefines "breach of security"** to include not just hacking but a **voluntary
unauthorized DISCLOSURE** of health data — i.e., a company sharing/selling health data to advertisers,
inconsistent with its promises, is *itself* a reportable breach.

**→ Why this matters for a small provider:** "I'm cash-pay, so HIPAA doesn't apply to me" is a documented
trap. Even outside HIPAA, the FTC Act §5 and the HBNR reach the *same* pixel/data-sharing conduct — and
the enforcement record proves it. A cash-pay therapist leaking intake data through a Meta pixel is in the
same jeopardy as a HIPAA-covered clinic, just under a different law.
